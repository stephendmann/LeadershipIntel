### The four-question framework leaders keep reaching for

Ikigai gets shared constantly in leadership and consulting circles — on whiteboards, retreat decks, and LinkedIn carousels. For good reason. It asks four deceptively simple questions:

- What do I love?
- What am I good at?
- What does the world need?
- What can I be paid for?

Find your answers to all four, and you've found your *ikigai* — your reason for being, your reason to get up in the morning.

That's a compelling promise for any leader at a crossroads: taking on a new role, repositioning a practice, rethinking what kind of work they want to do next. Before you put too much weight on it, though, it helps to understand both what it does well and where it quietly misleads.

---

### What each question is actually asking

**What do I love?**
This is your energy audit. What activities, topics, and modes of working make time disappear? Not what you *think* you should love — what genuinely sustains you, even when things get hard. For leaders, this often reveals a mismatch between what they're *doing* and what actually drives them.

**What am I good at?**
Your strengths, skills, and natural advantages — the things others seek your help with without being asked. The honest version of this question cuts through modesty and imposter syndrome alike. It points you toward work where you create disproportionate value, rather than constantly working against your grain.

**What does the world need?**
This turns the lens outward. What problems, gaps, or aspirations exist in your industry, organisation, or community that you feel drawn to address? For leaders, this is the contribution question — the difference between a career and a calling.

**What can I be paid for?**
The grounding question. Which of your skills or services are people and organisations actually willing to pay for, at a level that sustains the life you want? This is where idealism meets reality — and where many purpose frameworks go evasive.

---

### Where it works well

When you find options that sit at the overlap of all four questions, you're closer to work that combines passion, competence, contribution, and sustainability. For leaders, the framework is most useful when:

- **Positioning a niche or practice** — ikigai helps you articulate a "sweet spot" that is both marketable and meaningful
- **Navigating a pivot** — it gives structure to reflection at a career inflection point
- **Balancing passion with pragmatism** — it forces the economic reality question alongside the purpose question
- **Creating a simple narrative** — for a bio, a pitch, or a strategic conversation, the four-quadrant story is memorable and communicable

---

### Where it quietly misleads

The popular Venn-diagram version of ikigai is a Western adaptation, not an authentic rendering of the Japanese concept. The original idea is less about finding a grand unified purpose and more about the small, daily sources of meaning — a good conversation, a craft practised well, connection with people you care about. The diagram turns that into something more ambitious, and more problematic.

**The honest list of limitations:**

- It oversimplifies real-world constraints. Markets don't neatly reward what you're passionate about and good at. The overlap is often partial, provisional, and shifting.
- It's too static for fast-moving fields. In AI, leadership consulting, or any domain undergoing rapid change, your "what the world needs" and "what you can be paid for" can shift dramatically within 12 months.
- It doesn't tell you how to execute. Knowing your ikigai doesn't give you an audience, an offer, a content strategy, or a client pipeline. It's a map without a route.
- It can fuel perfectionism and analysis paralysis. Leaders who spend too long searching for the "perfect" intersection often delay the very experiments that would tell them what actually works.
- It underplays non-monetised meaning. Small habits, relationships, physical health, and personal hobbies are valid sources of ikigai in the original sense — but the four-quadrant model quietly demotes them by making career the frame.

---

### How to use it wisely

**Use it for hypotheses, not conclusions.**
Rather than treating your ikigai as a fixed answer, treat it as a working hypothesis: *"My current ikigai hypothesis: helping mid-market leaders integrate AI through strategy advisory and structured workshops."* Then test it with real offers, real conversations, and real feedback — and revise.

**Pair it with a strategy layer.**
Ikigai tells you *what* might be worth pursuing. It doesn't tell you *who* your audience is, what your offer ladder looks like, or what platforms and formats will reach the right people. You need market research and positioning frameworks alongside it — not instead of it.

**Keep the original spirit in view.**
The Japanese concept is less about one grand purpose and more about a life that holds meaning across multiple domains. Tend to the parts of your life that recharge you — the habits, relationships, and interests that aren't monetised — so your work sits inside a sustainable whole rather than carrying all the weight of your meaning.

---

### The bottom line

Ikigai is a useful starting point — a reflective lens for leaders rethinking their direction, niche, or narrative. It won't give you a strategy, and it shouldn't be mistaken for one. Use it to surface hypotheses. Then do the harder work of testing them.
